# Systems Thinking — Typology Reference

*Distilled from Sandeep Swadia, "How To Think SO Clearly People Assume You're Brilliant" (YouTube, video ID `mjTgkm-h__M`, published 2026-06-11, ~1.01M views at time of capture). Used as the "typology" reference companion to the entry skill.*

While the [mapping reference](./lenny-mapping.md) teaches you how to model a system, this reference teaches you to diagnose **what kind of system you're in** before you decide how to act. The central claim: most expensive mistakes in business come from treating one type of system as another.

## Why Most People Get Systems Wrong

Three recurring sources of confusion, per Swadia:

1. **They don't know what kind of system they're in.** Each system type has its own response protocol. Bringing the wrong protocol to the right system is the single biggest error.
2. **The Cobra Effect** — incentive traps. When you reward the wrong proxy, people optimize for the reward and against the goal. The system looks rational from inside each player's incentives, but irrational from outside.
3. **Delayed feedback loops** — actions whose consequences arrive far later than the reward. The classic case: cigarettes delivered satisfaction in seconds and damage in decades. Delayed feedback is the most under-weighted risk in strategy decisions.

## The Four System Types

| Type             | Cause↔effect               | Right response                  | State of cause↔effect link |
| ---------------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| **Clear**        | Obvious, repeatable        | Follow checklist, no improv     | Directly observable        |
| **Complicated**  | Exists, but hidden         | Slow down, find the right expert | Discoverable through analysis |
| **Complex**      | Known only in hindsight    | Run small experiments, adapt    | Emergent, only retrospective |
| **Chaotic**      | Broken, no pattern         | Act first, stabilize, then learn | Impossible to know         |

**Matching protocol to type matters more than cleverness.** Expert advice is essential in Complicated systems but useless in Complex ones. Checklists win in Clear systems and kill people in Chaotic ones (analysis paralysis).

## DART — A Diagnostic for "Which System Am I In?"

Real life doesn't come with system-type labels. Use **DART** to diagnose before you act.

- **D — Deconstruct.** Break the problem into sub-parts. Are the parts stable, or constantly shifting?
- **A — Analyze.** What is the cause↔effect relationship? (Use the table above — *this single question tells you which system you're in and therefore what to do next*.)
- **R — Recognize.** Have you seen this pattern before — in this system or another? Cross-system pattern recognition compounds.
- **T — Test.** Run the smallest safe experiment before committing fully. **Skip T in Chaotic systems** — there's no time.

The `A` step is the load-bearing one. If you remember nothing else from DART, remember to formally classify the cause↔effect relationship before choosing a response.

## Anchor Cases (Memory Hooks)

These four stories make abstract system types stick. Use them when explaining the typology.

### Van Halen's Brown M&M's → **Clear**
In the 1980s, Van Halen's 400-page tour contract famously required a bowl of M&M's backstage with every brown M&M removed. Rockstar nonsense? No — a cheap-to-inspect signal for a Clear system. If the band found brown M&M's, they knew instantly the venue hadn't read the contract carefully, which meant they had probably also missed something dangerous in the stage setup. One observable detail → state of the whole system.

### Hiring Pipeline Diagnosis → **Complicated**
Cause↔effect exists, but is buried. You can't see it from the surface — you need an expert (a recruiting lead, an analyst) to walk the system, map the steps, and find where the leak is. Worth slowing down. No improvising.

### New Product or Market Entry → **Complex**
You cannot know in advance what will work. Cause↔effect only emerges in hindsight. The right move is **small experiments + course-correct over time** — stay "directionally right, not precisely right." Expert opinion is of limited use because no one has run this exact system before.

### Tylenol Cyanide Crisis, 1982 → **Chaotic**
Seven people in Chicago died after taking cyanide-laced Tylenol capsules. Nobody knew how widespread the danger was, which bottles were safe, or whether more deaths were coming. Johnson & Johnson had no time for analysis or experts or checklists. They went into crisis mode: warned the public, pulled 31 million bottles off shelves. **Strategy: stabilize first, ask later, understand later.** The biggest mistake in chaos is analysis paralysis — chaos has no interest in teaching anyone anything.

### Bonus: British Cobra Bounties → **Incentive Trap**
Early 1900s, British officials in Delhi paid a bounty for every dead cobra to reduce the population. Result: people started *breeding* cobras to collect more bounties. The policy designed to reduce the population ended up increasing it. Lesson: **every player responds to their own incentives, not to your goal.** This is why the mapping reference insists on the *players-and-incentives* step before any intervention.

### Bonus: Cigarettes → **Delayed Feedback**
For most of the 20th century, smoking was ubiquitous — restaurants, airplanes, offices. Satisfaction arrived in seconds (relief, focus, social bonding). Damage arrived in decades. Not one cigarette → one cancer; rather, a slow compounding stock. Lesson: **when the reward is fast and the cost is slow, the system will mislead anyone judging only by first-order feedback.**
